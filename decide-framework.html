DECIDE Framework

Structured approach for planning and conducting the KidDose application evaluation.

The DECIDE Framework

The DECIDE framework is a structured approach for planning and conducting usability evaluations. It provides a systematic way to evaluate interactive systems, ensuring that all important aspects of the evaluation are considered.

Application Context

This framework was used to guide our usability testing of the KidDose medication reminder application, helping us structure our approach to identifying usability issues and recommending improvements.

D
Determine the goals
Introduction

This usability testing activity aims to evaluate a prototype mobile application designed to help users manage their medication schedules. The app allows users to scan the name of their medicine, input dosage frequency, duration, and specific times, and receive timely reminders through alarms.

Goals
  • To examine the usability and user experience of the medication reminder app among student users.
  • To assess the clarity and effectiveness of the app's form-filling, dashboard display, and alarm features.
  • To evaluate the overall intuitiveness and visual presentation of the user interface.
Key Focus:

Our evaluation focused on assessing how effectively users could complete key tasks like signing up, scanning medicine, and changing their password, while measuring performance metrics and user satisfaction.

Step 1
E
Explore the questions
Pre-Test Interview Questions

To understand participants' medication habits and the relevance of the app, we asked:

  • Have you been sick before?
  • What medicine did you take?
  • Do you take any medicine regularly?
  • Do you currently use anything to remind you to take your medicine?
  • How often do you forget to take your medication?
Application Interaction Questions

These questions guided our task-based usability assessment:

  • Can the user successfully sign up for an account?
  • Can the user scan a medicine and accurately input dosage frequency, duration, and time?
  • Can the user find the added medicine entry in the dashboard?
  • Can the user successfully change their account password?
Question Strategy:

Our questions were designed to first establish the participants' context and needs, then evaluate how well the app addressed those needs through specific task performance.

Step 2
C
Choose the evaluation methods

We conducted in-person usability testing with participants from the BS in Information Technology program. Since our classes were asynchronous and not held on campus, the testing sessions took place off-campus in quiet, low-distraction environments such as peaceful study corners and low-traffic indoor spaces. Each participant interacted with the app using a mobile prototype run on a laptop, allowing better visibility and smoother facilitation of the tasks.

Before testing, we explained the purpose and features of the app and asked them to sign a video consent form. We then guided them through three main tasks: signing up, scanning a medicine and entering details, and changing their password.

The usability testing team consisted of:
  • 1 Facilitator – explained the tasks and observed behavior
  • 1 Note-taker – recorded observations and feedback
  • 1 Timekeeper – measured how long each task took
  • 1 Video recorder – captured testing sessions for later analysis
Testing Environment:

By conducting tests in quiet environments with a laptop-based prototype, we were able to closely observe user interactions and gather detailed feedback despite the limitations of not being able to test on actual mobile devices.

Step 3
I
Identify practical issues
Participants

Six undergraduate BS in Information Technology students who had recently been sick and were familiar with taking medicine participated in the usability test. All were comfortable using mobile applications and owned Android smartphones. Although the prototype was designed for mobile devices, the test was conducted using the mobile version of the prototype in Figma on a laptop. This approach allowed for better visibility and smoother facilitation during testing, especially since sessions were held off-campus due to asynchronous classes and conducted in quiet, low-distraction environments.

Participant Selection:

Our participants were ideal for testing the medication reminder app as they all had recent experience with taking medication and shared a common challenge of forgetting to take their medicine, which is precisely the problem our app aims to solve.

Step 4
D
Decide how to deal with ethical issues
  • All participants signed a video and participation consent form prior to testing.
  • The test's purpose and process were explained clearly.
  • Personal information was handled with confidentiality.
  • Participants were informed of their right to withdraw at any time.
  • No identifying data was used in the analysis or reports.

Step 5
E
Evaluate, interpret, and present the data

To assess the system's usability, we developed an evaluation form aimed at measuring three key aspects: user performance, satisfaction level, and perceived task complexity. We utilized a usability specification table to structure the evaluation and recorded statistical data for each participant based on their interaction with the prototype. This approach enabled us to gather quantifiable insights on how effectively users completed tasks, how satisfied they were with the experience, and how difficult they found each task.
